# Case Study: Cyberbullying in India — Kalandi Charan Lenka v. State of Odisha  
## 1.What is Cyberbullying?  
Cyberbullying is repeated, intentional harassment, threats, or humiliation carried out through digital platforms such as social media, messaging apps, or online games, often targeting someone’s reputation, privacy, or emotional well‑being.    

In India, one notable real incident of cyberbullying was the Kalandi Charan Lenka case heard by the Orissa High Court in 2017. In this incident, the victim, a young woman studying at Pattamundai Women’s College, began receiving obscene and defamatory messages on her mobile phone that questioned her character and reputation. These messages caused significant emotional distress to her and her family. The situation escalated when a fake Facebook account was created in her name, and morphed explicit photos of her were uploaded with the intention of defaming her and humiliating her socially.    

## 2.How Cyberbullying Typically Happens  
**Step 1:Identification of the Target** The bully selects a person—often someone they know from school, college, or social media. Targets are usually teenagers or young adults who are active online.  
**Step 2:Access Through Digital Platforms** The bully gains access to the victim through platforms like Instagram, WhatsApp, Facebook, gaming chats, or school/college groups. Sometimes they use fake accounts.  
**Step 3:Sending Hurtful or Harassing Messages** The bully begins with insulting texts, mocking comments, or threatening messages. This may happen privately (DMs) or publicly through comments or posts.  
**Step 4:Spreading Rumors or Fake Information** The bully may post false statements, edited photos, or misleading content to damage the victim’s image. This is often shared with classmates or the public.  
**Step 5:Public Humiliation** The bully may tag the victim in embarrassing posts, make memes, or share screenshots to humiliate them in front of a wider audience.  
**Step 6:Isolation and Repetition** Cyberbullying usually continues repeatedly. The victim may receive multiple messages, posts, or harassment across different apps.  
**Step 7:Escalation to Identity Misuse** In more serious cases, the bully creates fake accounts, impersonates the victim, or posts content pretending to be them to damage their reputation.  

## 3. Target Audience  
Victims of cyberbullying in India are often young adults and students, especially women, whose reputations can be targeted through social media.  

## 4. Consequences  
The emotional impact included mental trauma, social embarrassment, and disruption to her education. Legally, the accused faced investigation under sections of the Information Technology Act (related to identity theft and posting obscene content), showing how cyberbullying can lead to criminal charges and court action in India.
